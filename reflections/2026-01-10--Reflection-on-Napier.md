&nbsp; # On the Trivialization of Disaster Analysis



&nbsp; ## Raw Reflection

&nbsp; Analysis of japanese media as reactions to disaster has become almost trite as the western academic study of japanese media has further developed. There's something to be said about the neolibral fascination with disaster narratives. As discussed by Mark Fisher, it is easier to imagine a future of disaster than it is to imagine the end of a neolibral order.

&nbsp; - Trigger: Reading/processing Napier's "Panic Sites" article

&nbsp; - Mood: Critical, somewhat dismissive but engaged

&nbsp; - Surface: (not specified)

&nbsp; ## Initial Interpretation



&nbsp; This reflection captures a moment of critical distance from a well-established scholarly framework. The use of "trite" suggests both familiarity with the disaster-analysis approach and impatience with its ubiquity in Western Japanese media studies.



&nbsp; The invocation of Mark Fisher's capitalist realism shifts the frame—suggesting that the "Japanese fascination with disaster" discourse may itself be symptomatic of neoliberal epistemological limits. If disaster is easier to imagine than systemic change, then perhaps Western academics gravitate toward disaster analysis because it requires no fundamental interrogation of the order producing both the scholarship and the films.



&nbsp; The reflection doesn't resolve whether Napier's analysis remains valuable despite becoming conventional, or whether Fisher's framework subsumes/supersedes the cultural-national reading Napier offers.



&nbsp; The typo ("neolibral") and incomplete thought ("There's something to be said about...") preserved as indicators of the note's immediacy.
