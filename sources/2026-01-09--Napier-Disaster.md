# Panic Sites: The Japanese Imagination of Disaster from Godzilla to Akira

## Bibliographic Information
**Author:** Susan J. Napier
**Title:** Panic Sites: The Japanese Imagination of Disaster from Godzilla to Akira
**Publication:** Journal of Japanese Studies, Vol. 19, No. 2 (Summer, 1993), pp. 327-351
**Publisher:** The Society for Japanese Studies
**Stable URL:** http://www.jstor.org/stable/132643

## Key Arguments

### Central Thesis
Japanese dystopian/disaster science fiction films reveal evolving conceptions of Japanese national identity across three decades (1950s-1980s), tracking from nuclear anxiety and moral certainty (Godzilla, 1954) through ambivalence about success (Nippon chinbotsu, 1974) to the celebration of destruction and erasure of history (Akira, 1989).

### Major Claims

**On Genre Evolution:**
- Movement from "secure horror" (definitive closure, collectivity threatened from outside but restored) in Godzilla and Nippon chinbotsu to postmodern privileging of narrative movement and lack of closure in Akira
- Shift from negative portrayal of disaster toward virtual celebration of it
- Represents generational change in conception of Japan's identity

**On Historical Relationship:**
- Godzilla: attempts to rewrite history (compensatory reimagining of wartime trauma)
- Nippon chinbotsu: attempts to enshrine history (elegiac mourning for traditional culture)
- Akira: largely successful effort to erase history

**On Cultural Context:**
- Science fiction as particularly appropriate vehicle for examining Japanese modernization anxieties
- Genre's vocabulary (technological advancement, progress, the machine) reflects instrumentalities of modern capitalism
- Japanese SF tends toward darker, more nihilistic visions than Western counterparts

### Specific Film Analyses

**Godzilla (1954):**
- Demonizes American nuclear science (reference to Hiroshima/Nagasaki)
- "Good" Japanese science triumphs, offering cathartic and compensatory experience
- Operates as "secure horror" where successful human intervention is possible
- Allows postwar audience to rewrite/reimagine tragic wartime experiences

**Nippon chinbotsu (1974):**
- Four-hour depiction of Japan's total submersion into ocean
- Enormous domestic hit, failed internationally
- Tone of mourning rather than excitement
- Japan personified as female victim with no hope of recovery
- Reflects 1973 identity: insecurity about social disintegration combined with pride in traditional collectivity
- Context: Komatsu Sakyo (b. 1931) generation traumatized by war, experiencing ambivalence during economic success
- "Freeze-frame" of Japanese citizenry combining insecurity with cultural fear of success
- Ends with high-altitude shot of empty ocean with superimposed city names—history encased in memory alone

**Akira (1989):**
- "Tech noir" aesthetic: dark, hard-edged visual vocabulary
- Neo Tokyo as defamiliarization of present Japanese society (not future extrapolation)
- Highlights contemporary problems: aimless youth, repression, new religions
- "Genuinely novelistic density of incident and character"
- Celebrates history's imminent demise
- Most vividly realized evocation of disaster in Japanese cinema
- Highest grossing film in Japan (1989), strong international reception

## Context Provided

### On Japanese Popular Cinema
- Long ignored by Western critics who focused on art house directors (Kurosawa, Ozu, Oshima)
- Dismissed as "plethora of nudity, teenage heroes, science-fiction monsters, animated cartoons, and pictures about cute animals" (Donald Richie)
- Recent critical re-evaluation, especially of animated science fiction
- Cult following both domestically and internationally

### On Science Fiction as Cultural Mirror
Cites Fredric Jameson: SF serves to "defamiliarize and restructure our experience of our own present"

Rise of SF "corresponds to the waning or blockage of...historicity...to its crisis and paralysis" — particularly relevant to post-Meiji Japan's attempt to "destroy or at least rewrite history"

## Initial Interpretation

This article provides a framework for understanding disaster imagery as historical/cultural symptom rather than mere spectacle. Napier traces how the "imagination of disaster" reflects shifting anxieties about Japanese identity across postwar decades.

The progression she identifies—from compensatory rewriting (Godzilla) through elegiac preservation (Nippon chinbotsu) to celebratory erasure (Akira)—suggests not just aesthetic evolution but fundamental shifts in how Japanese culture relates to its own continuity and memory.

Her observation about generational differences is particularly striking: each film emerges from and speaks to different cohorts' relationship with trauma, success, and futurity.

The concept of "secure horror" versus "critical dystopia" offers useful vocabulary for understanding narrative closure as ideological gesture—what it means when disaster can or cannot be contained.
